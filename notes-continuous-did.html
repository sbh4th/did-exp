Notes on Continuous Treatment DiD and Air Pollution (PM₂.₅)

Compiled from a reading of Callaway, Goodman-Bacon & Sant’Anna (2025) and related literature

1. What PM₂.₅ Actually Measures

PM₂.₅ is a gravimetric mass measurement: ambient air is pulled through a size-selective inlet (cutting particles >2.5 µm aerodynamic diameter) onto a filter, weighed before and after. The result is µg/m³ of total particle mass. It is deliberately agnostic about chemical composition.

Do the Components “Add Up”?

In principle yes — by conservation of mass the chemical components should sum to total gravimetric mass. In practice they systematically do not, and the shortfall is informative. Typical reconstruction:

Component Contribution
Sulfate (SO₄²⁻) ~20–40%
Nitrate (NO₃⁻) ~10–20%
Organic carbon (OC) ~20–40%
Elemental carbon (EC) ~5–10%
Crustal/mineral dust ~5–15%
Sea salt ~1–5%
Trace metals (Ni, Cu, V, Zn, Mn, K…) ~1–5%
Reconstructed total ~80–110% of gravimetric mass

Key sources of discrepancy: - Water/hydration: hygroscopic salts retain water even after filter conditioning - Organic mass multiplier: labs measure organic carbon only; a multiplier (1.4–2.1) converts OC → organic mass (OM). Getting this wrong propagates large errors - Unmeasured species: ammonium (NH₄⁺ is a key counterion to sulfate/nitrate), many trace organics - Volatilization artifacts: semi-volatile species (esp. ammonium nitrate) evaporate off filters post-collection - Positive artifacts: gas-phase organics adsorb onto filters during sampling

How Composition Is Determined

  • Filter-based speciation (EPA CSN/IMPROVE networks): multiple filter splits sent to different labs for ion chromatography, XRF, and thermal-optical OC/EC analysis. Gives 24-hour time-averaged snapshots
  • Source apportionment (receptor modeling): EPA PMF (Positive Matrix Factorization) decomposes a long time-series of speciated data into latent source factors. If Cu, Fe, and Ba co-vary across days, they likely share a source (e.g., brake wear)
  • Online/continuous instruments: ACSM gives near-real-time organics, sulfate, nitrate, ammonium; aethalometers measure black carbon
  • Isotopic/molecular markers: levoglucosan for biomass burning, hopanes for motor oil, etc.

Health Research Implications

If PM₂.₅ mass is just a proxy for some causally toxic component, health effect estimates based on total mass will be attenuated. Studies (Six Cities work onward) suggest transition metals, secondary sulfate, and combustion EC/BC show stronger associations than crustal material — but separating these statistically is difficult due to correlation and measurement error.

2. The DiD Revolution and Continuous Treatments

Background: The Staggered Binary Treatment Problem

The “revolution” in DiD (circa 2018–2023) showed that two-way fixed effects (TWFE) regressions with staggered binary treatments estimate non-convex — potentially sign-reversed — weighted averages of group-time treatment effects. The key papers: Goodman-Bacon (2021), Callaway & Sant’Anna (2021), de Chaisemartin & D’Haultfoeuille (2020), Sun & Abraham (2021), Borusyak, Jaravel & Spiess (2023).

Extending to Continuous Treatments

Most of the revolution focused on binary treatments. Can the same logic apply to a continuous treatment like PM₂.₅? Yes, but the problems are even deeper.

3. The Core Paper: Callaway, Goodman-Bacon & Sant’Anna (2025)

Full citation: Brantly Callaway, Andrew Goodman-Bacon, and Pedro H.C. Sant’Anna, “Difference-in-Differences with a Continuous Treatment,” arXiv 2107.02637v8, December 2025. R package: contdid (on CRAN).

3.1 Two Types of Treatment Effects — A Critical Distinction

With a continuous treatment, there are two fundamentally different objects:

Level treatment effects (dose-response function): - Yᵢ(d) − Yᵢ(0): the effect of dose d relative to no treatment - “What is the total causal effect of receiving dose d compared to no treatment?” - Estimand: ATT(d|d) = average level effect on units that actually received dose d

Causal responses (derivatives): - Y′ᵢ(d): the derivative of potential outcomes with respect to dose - “What is the marginal effect of a small increase in dose at level d?” - Estimand: ACRT(d|d) = average causal response at dose d for units receiving dose d

These coincide when treatment is binary but are genuinely different with continuous treatment. A large ATT(d|d) can coexist with near-zero or negative ACRT(d|d) — the policy had large total effects but further dose increases do little or harm. Confusing these leads to badly wrong policy conclusions.

3.2 What Parallel Trends Buys You (and Doesn’t)

Theorem 3.1 (Good news): Under standard parallel trends (untreated potential outcomes trend the same across dose groups), ATT(d|d) is identified:

ATT(d|d) = E[ΔY | D=d] − E[ΔY | D=0]

The dose-response level function is nonparametrically identified. This is the continuous analogue of the binary DiD ATT.

Theorem 3.2 (Bad news): Under standard parallel trends, causal responses are NOT identified. Comparing outcome trends between adjacent dose groups gives:

E[ΔY|D=h] − E[ΔY|D=l] = ACRT(h|h) + selection bias

where the selection bias = AT T(l|h) − AT T(l|l) — the difference in treatment effects at the same dose across different dose groups. Parallel trends doesn’t restrict how treatment effects vary across groups, so this contamination is not ruled out. You’re jumping from one ATT function to a different group’s ATT function, and the slope of that jump is not the slope of either function.

Strong Parallel Trends (Assumption 5): To identify causal responses, a stronger assumption is needed: the average outcome evolution for the whole population if everyone received dose d equals the observed path of dose-d units. This implies ATT(d|d) = ATE(d) for all doses — no selection into dose groups on the basis of treatment effect heterogeneity. Under SPT, causal responses are identified. Crucially, pre-treatment tests cannot distinguish standard PT from SPT, because SPT restricts treated potential outcomes (never observed pre-treatment).

3.3 TWFE Is Even More Broken Than You Think

The standard TWFE regression:

Yᵢₜ = θₜ + ηᵢ + β^twfe · Dᵢ · Postₜ + vᵢₜ

can be decomposed in four different ways depending on the “building block” parameter used. None yield a clean causal interpretation:

  • As a weighted average of ATT(d|d): Weights integrate to zero, not one — β^twfe literally cannot be interpreted as an average treatment effect. It effectively runs a binary DiD using higher-dose units as “treated” and lower-dose as “control.”
  • As scaled ATT: Weights integrate to one but include negative values.
  • As causal response: Weights are non-negative but there is a selection bias term plus weights differ from the dose density.
  • As 2×2 effects: Similar problems.

These TWFE pathologies arise even with only two time periods — unlike binary staggered case issues, which require multiple periods. The problems are fundamentally about continuous treatment heterogeneity, not staggering.

3.4 Proposed Estimators

Building on Chen, Christensen & Kankanala (2023):

  • For ATT(d|d): Nonparametric sieve estimator of E[ΔY|D=d] − E[ΔY|D=0]. Achieves minimax optimal convergence rate in sup-norm; uniform confidence bands narrower than undersmoothing while maintaining correct coverage.
  • For ACRT(d|d): Derivative of E[ΔY|D=d] with respect to d under Strong PT.
  • For summary scalars:
    • ATTₒ: run a binary DiD with treatment dummy = 1 for any D > 0. Correctly uses dose density as weights.
    • ACRTₒ: estimate average derivative via “flexible” linear regression.

3.5 What “Post” Means — And Why It Doesn’t Apply to PM₂.₅

Equation 1.1 requires a specific design: Dᵢ × Postₜ — a cross-product of a fixed dose variable and a binary time switch. This presupposes: - Dᵢ is time-invariant (measured at baseline, pre-event) - Postₜ is a binary switch at a well-defined calendar event (e.g., a regulatory reform) - Before the event, everyone is untreated; the dose “activates” after it

This is the differential exposure DiD design: a policy event happens; units differ in pre-determined exposure to it; you trace outcome dynamics before and after.

For PM₂.₅, none of this holds: - There is no “Post” — no well-defined event that turns the dose “on” - The dose Dᵢₜ is time-varying — the same monitor location has different PM₂.₅ readings year to year - Dᵢ · Postₜ cannot be formed when Dᵢ is itself time-varying

The paper’s core framework (main body, Sections 3–5) is built for the differential exposure design. PM₂.₅ as a purely observational environmental exposure is a structurally different problem.

4. The De Chaisemartin et al. Framework for Always-On Continuous Treatments

The Stayers/Switchers Setup

De Chaisemartin, D’Haultfoeuille, Pasquier, Sow & Vazquez-Bare (2025), “Difference-in-Differences for Continuous Treatments and Instruments with Stayers.” arXiv 2201.06898v8, August 2025. Stata/R package: did_multiplegt_stat.

This paper is designed for the case where treatment Dᵢₜ varies over time for every unit — taxes, tariffs, prices. No “Post” required. Instead: - Switchers: units whose dose changes between consecutive periods - Stayers: units whose dose stays the same - DiD comparison: switchers vs. stayers with the same baseline dose, comparing outcome evolutions

The parallel trends assumption: switchers and stayers with the same period-one treatment would have experienced the same average outcome evolution if the switchers’ treatment had not changed. This is testable via pre-trend placebo tests when a period-zero is available.

Target parameters: Average Of Switchers’ Slopes (AOSS) — the average slope of switchers’ period-two potential outcome function, from their period-one to period-two treatment. This is a local effect on switchers, not a global dose-response.

The PM₂.₅ Problem: No Stayers

The stayers assumption requires units whose treatment doesn’t change between consecutive periods. For ambient PM₂.₅ driven by meteorology and emissions, there are essentially no stayers at any fine time scale. Every county’s PM₂.₅ varies year to year. The de Chaisemartin et al. papers acknowledge this explicitly.

The “Quasi-Stayers” Rescue

The key theoretical innovation for the no-stayers case is quasi-stayers — units whose treatment change is infinitesimally small even if not exactly zero. The estimator takes the limit of stayer outcome changes as treatment change approaches zero:

Counterfactual trend ≈ E[ΔY | D₁ = d₁, ΔD → 0]

estimated nonparametrically. For PM₂.₅ this is more defensible than true stayers: some counties will have PM₂.₅ that barely moves year-to-year (far from emission sources, stable meteorology), while others have large swings. The quasi-stayer estimator uses the former as a local nonparametric control for the latter, conditioning on the same baseline concentration level.

The assumption is testable: you can run a pre-trend test comparing period-zero-to-one outcome evolutions of units that will become quasi-stayers vs. switchers.

The No-Stayers Extension

De Chaisemartin, D’Haultfoeuille & Vazquez-Bare (2024), “Difference-in-Difference Estimators with Continuous Treatments and No Stayers,” AEA Papers and Proceedings 114: 610–13. Explicitly addresses the temperature/precipitation case where all units’ treatments change.

5. The Sun & Shapiro Impossibility Result

Liyang Sun and Jesse M. Shapiro, “A Linear Panel Model with Heterogeneous Coefficients and Variation in Exposure,” Journal of Economic Perspectives 36(4): 193–204, 2022.

Key result: TWFE can fail to estimate the average (or even a weighted average) of units’ coefficients under treatment effect heterogeneity. More starkly: under some conditions, there exists no estimator that is guaranteed to estimate even a weighted average — this is a nonparametric identification impossibility result, not just a statement about TWFE bias.

The positive rescue: when there is a unit totally unaffected by treatment (D=0 always), it becomes possible to estimate an average effect by replacing TWFE with an average of DiD estimators. For PM₂.₅, that zero-exposure unit does not exist.

This means that for PM₂.₅ as a purely observational time-varying exposure with no policy event and no instrument, no estimator can be guaranteed to recover even a weighted average of causal effects under full treatment effect heterogeneity.

6. What Can Actually Be Done? The Toolkit

Option 1: Quasi-Stayer/No-Stayers DiD (de Chaisemartin et al.)

The closest to a design-based answer without an instrument. Requires: - Quasi-stayers exist (counties with near-zero year-to-year PM₂.₅ changes) - Their outcome trends are valid counterfactuals for counties with larger changes, conditional on the same baseline dose

This is a conditional parallel trends assumption that is empirically testable. The causal interpretation is local to switchers (marginal effect of PM₂.₅ change), which may actually be the policy-relevant parameter.

Important note on software implementation — there are three distinct scenarios with different packages:

Scenario Package Notes
Continuous Dᵢₜ, stayers exist did_multiplegt_stat Requires that some units’ treatment genuinely doesn’t change between periods
All units go from D=0 to D>0, no untreated control did_had (Stata: ssc install did_had) Heterogeneous adoption design; uses quasi-stayers as controls
Fully always-on continuous Dᵢₜ, no stayers No dedicated package yet Theory exists in de Chaisemartin et al. (2024) AEA P&P; replication code available but no canned estimator

For ambient PM₂.₅, the relevant case is the third row. did_multiplegt_stat explicitly requires stayers — its entire estimation logic (regression adjustment or propensity-score reweighting of stayers’ outcome changes) depends on stayers being present. The quasi-stayers/no-stayers estimator for always-on continuous treatments exists in theory and in a replication package, but has not yet been rolled into a turnkey command.

Option 2: Instrumentation (Most Credible)

Uses variation in pollution that is plausibly exogenous to health outcomes. Key instruments in the literature:

Instrument Mechanism Papers
Wind direction PM₂.₅ transported from upwind emission sources Deschenes, Greenstone, and others
Wildfire smoke Exogenous PM₂.₅ shock from distant fires Burke et al.
Thermal inversions Meteorological trapping of local PM₂.₅ Various
NAAQS nonattainment Regulatory threshold creates sharp dose change RD/IV literature

The de Chaisemartin et al. framework has an explicit IV extension — you can use wind direction as an instrument for PM₂.₅ in the stayers/switchers framework and recover a Wald-type estimator of the causal response slope.

Option 3: Regression Discontinuity at Regulatory Thresholds

NAAQS nonattainment designations create sharp thresholds in regulatory treatment. Counties just above a PM₂.₅ standard face mandatory abatement plans; counties just below do not. This generates a plausible discontinuity in regulatory treatment, which serves as a first stage for ambient PM₂.₅ changes. No parallel trends assumption required — identification is from the discontinuity.

Option 4: Dose-Density Reweighted TWFE (If You Must)

If none of the above designs are available, the Callaway et al. contribution suggests replacing the variance-weighted TWFE with the dose-density-weighted summary measures they propose. Specifically: - For ATTₒ: binary DiD with treatment dummy = 1 for D > 0 - For ACRTₒ: average derivative via flexible linear regression

This doesn’t solve the parallel trends problem but ensures you’re estimating something with interpretable weights — a genuine average of unit-level effects under the maintained assumptions, rather than a badly-weighted mix.

Option 5: Synthetic Control / Matrix Completion

For specific large pollution events (plant opening/closing, regulation change), synthetic control methods construct unit-specific counterfactuals from a donor pool, relaxing the parallel trends assumption at the cost of requiring a long pre-treatment period for fitting. Newer matrix completion methods (Athey et al., Bai & Ng) generalize this to arbitrary panel factor structures.

7. Summary Assessment

Design Identifying Assumption PM₂.₅ Applicability
TWFE with Dᵢ·Postₜ Parallel trends + treatment effect homogeneity Requires a discrete policy event activating dose
Callaway et al. (2025) Parallel trends (PT or Strong PT) + a “Post” event Same — designed for differential exposure, not ambient levels
De Chaisemartin stayers Conditional PT for switchers vs. stayers with same baseline Requires quasi-stayers; strong but testable assumption
Sun-Shapiro impossibility — Without D=0 units or an instrument, no estimator guaranteed
IV (wind, smoke, inversions) Exclusion restriction + relevance Most credible for ambient PM₂.₅; identifies LATE
RD at nonattainment threshold Continuity at threshold Identifies local effect at regulatory threshold
Synthetic control No interference + pre-trend fit Good for specific large events, not general ambient variation

The Honest Bottom Line

The recent DiD revolution has made things harder for ambient pollution research — not in the sense of making estimation harder in practice, but by clarifying what assumptions researchers were previously getting away with implicitly. For PM₂.₅ as a purely observational time-varying exposure with no policy event and no instrument:

  • The differential-exposure DiD (Callaway et al.) is not applicable — there is no “Post”
  • The stayers/switchers framework (de Chaisemartin et al.) can be applied but requires the quasi-stayers assumption, which is substantive
  • The Sun-Shapiro impossibility result means that under full treatment effect heterogeneity, no estimator can guarantee recovering even a weighted average causal effect

The most credible work on PM₂.₅ health effects will continue to rely on instruments (wind direction, wildfire smoke, regulatory discontinuities) rather than DiD-type parallel trends arguments. DiD-type methods can be made to work but require very carefully defended conditional parallel trends assumptions about what drives year-to-year variation in PM₂.₅.
